## Experience is designed, not delivered

Forlizzi and Battarbee (2004) help us see that experience is not just satisfaction, usability, cognitive performance, or task success.

Experience emerges through interactions between:
- people
- products
- situations
- social interpretation
- shared meaning

Project question:
- What kind of encounter is your system staging?

---

## Three types of user-product interaction

Forlizzi and Battarbee give us useful markers:

- **Fluent interaction**
  - use becomes smooth, automatic, backgrounded
- **Cognitive interaction**
  - the product becomes something to figure out
- **Expressive interaction**
  - the user personalizes, adapts, narrates, or forms attachment

Question:
- Which type of interaction does your project mostly invite?

---

## Three types of experience

They also distinguish:

- **Experience**
  - the ongoing stream of everyday interaction
- **An experience**
  - a bounded, memorable event
- **Co-experience**
  - meaning and emotion made socially around or through use

This is especially useful for final projects because it gives students a way to describe the project as more than a tool.

---

## Co-experience and AI

Co-experience matters because meaning is often made socially.

People:
- talk about them
- compare experiences
- develop shared interpretations
- form norms around them
- decide together what the interaction meant

Human-AI question:
- Do human-AI interactions create **shared meaning**?
- Can we have a **social relation** with an AI system?
- Or are we always relating through the AI to designers, institutions, datasets, platforms, and other people?

## AI relation is a design choice

Zhang et al. (2024) argue that AI assistants are not only experienced as neutral tools.

Users may relate to them through recognizable relationship types:

- **master-servant**
- **partnership**

The relationship type matters because it can shape:
- social support
- emotional support
- subjective well-being

---

## Master-servant vs partnership

**Master-servant**
- command
- obedience
- control
- efficiency
- AI as instrument

**Partnership**
- cooperation
- support
- mutual orientation
- emotional presence
- AI as relational participant

---

## Relation is designed

Relationship type is not just a user interpretation.

It is shaped by design choices:
- system prompt
- tone
- memory
- interface framing
- turn-taking
- error handling
- what the system asks of the user
- what the system refuses to do

The project is always saying something about what the AI is.

---

## How have you shaped your AI?

Many people already use custom instructions to shape the social relation.

Examples of things people specify:
- expertise level
- tone
- directness
- warmth
- patience
- whether to challenge them
- whether to act like a tutor, peer, editor, coach, critic, or colleague

Discussion prompt:
- What have you told GPT about how it should talk to you?

## Emotional texture and risk

Li and Zhang (2024) add an important caution:

Human-AI relation can be emotionally real, socially meaningful, and unstable.

AI interaction can involve:
- intimacy
- mundane exchange
- self-disclosure
- play and fantasy
- customization
- transgression
- breakdown

Project question:
- What emotional texture might this system produce?

---

## Synthesis

Across the readings, a project can be described through three questions:

1. **Experience**
   - What does the system feel like over time, as a moment, or with others?
2. **Relation**
   - What role does the user inhabit, and what role does the AI perform?
3. **Consequence**
   - What support, attachment, unease, dependency, ambiguity, or shared meaning might follow?
