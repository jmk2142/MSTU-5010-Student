# Week 12
## Expression and Co-Creation

**What kind of expression is being supported or transformed, and what should remain human in that process?**

---

## Opening prompt

Think of **one moment** in your project process when you felt genuinely creative while working with AI.

- What were you trying to make?
- What did the AI actually contribute?
- Why did that moment feel creative to you?

---

## Why these readings together

- **Shneiderman**
  - gives a design language for creativity support
- **Rafner et al.**
  - update that question for the generative AI moment
- **Together**
  - help us distinguish support, co-creation, and substitution

---

## Shneiderman's baseline

Creativity is not magic.

It is structured, social, and tool-mediated.

Four recurring activities:
- collect
- relate
- create
- donate

Key design question:
- How does a system help people create more capably without replacing the creator?

---

<!-- _class: rafner-tight -->

## Rafner et al.'s shift

The question is no longer only:
- Can AI be creative?

The more useful question is:
- How should we understand **co-creativity**?

Four C model:
- `mini-c`: personal meaning-making
- `little-c`: everyday creativity
- `pro-c`: expert creativity
- `big-C`: field-changing creativity

Important implication:
- the human contribution can become harder to see in AI-assisted creative work

---

## What should remain human?

Possible answers:
- framing the purpose
- setting constraints
- selecting and revising outputs
- taking responsibility for style and consequences
- making the authorship claim

The key issue is not whether AI participates.

The key issue is whether human intention and control remain legible.

---

## A useful distinction

- **Support**
  - the system helps a human do creative work
- **Co-create**
  - the system materially shapes options and process
- **Substitute**
  - the system does so much that the human becomes mainly an approver or curator

Question:
- At what point does support slide into substitution?

---

## Two open questions for discussion

1. What part of creative work should AI support, and what part should remain unmistakably human?
2. If authorship is distributed across a human and a generative system, what kind of expressive claim can we still defend?
