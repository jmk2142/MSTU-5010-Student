# Week 11 - Interpretation and Meaning-Making

## Core Question
**What kinds of meaning-making and interpretation should remain human?**

## Reading Assignment
Read the following before class:

1. Gaver, W. W., Beaver, J., and Benford, S. (2003). *Ambiguity as a resource for design.*
2. Kress, G., and Selander, S. (2012). *Multimodal design, learning and cultures of recognition.*
3. Dorigoni, A., and Giardino, P. L. (2025). *The illusion of empathy: Evaluating AI-generated outputs in moments that matter.*

## Reading Focus
This week widens the conversation beyond problem-solving alone.
It asks how systems frame meaning, how people interpret outputs and interfaces, and what kinds of ambiguity should remain open rather than resolved too quickly.

## What To Look For
As you read, identify four things:

1. The interpretive move
- How do people make meaning from forms, outputs, interfaces, or interactions?

2. The role of framing
- How does the design of a system guide, narrow, or open interpretation?

3. The value of ambiguity
- When does ambiguity support reflection, interpretation, or critical engagement rather than confusion?

4. A project implication
- What meanings should your project invite, complicate, or leave open?

## Crucial Questions
Come to class ready to discuss:

1. What meanings does this system invite?
2. What meanings should remain open rather than fully resolved?
3. How does the system frame the user's interpretation?
4. Is the project trying to explain, provoke, destabilize, or reframe?
5. What happens when people attribute understanding, empathy, or intention to an AI system?

## Weekly Structure
Each week in this part of the course, you do the same three things:

1. Complete the assigned readings.
2. Come prepared to discuss them.
3. Bring a short `Project Throughline` update.

## Project Throughline
Bring a short weekly update that responds to these prompts:

- `This week's question for my project is/was...`
- `One design decision I made because of it is/was...`
- `One thing I tested, sketched, or represented is/was...`
- `What changed in my project thinking is/was...`

This does not need to be code.
It can be a sketch, diagram, prompt flow, prototype, comparison, storyboard, or another concrete representation of your thinking.

## Possible Outcome
By the end of class, you should have a stronger sense of what your project means, how it frames interpretation, and what it intentionally leaves open.
