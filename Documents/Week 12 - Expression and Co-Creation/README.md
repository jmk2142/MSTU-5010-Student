# Week 12 - Expression and Co-Creation

## Core Question
**How can AI expand or complicate human expression without erasing human intention?**

## Reading Assignment
Read the following before class:

1. Shneiderman, B. (2002). *Creativity support tools.*
2. Rafner, J., Beaty, R. E., Kaufman, J. C., Lubart, T., and Sherson, J. (2023). *Creativity in the age of generative AI.*

Course readings folder: [Google Drive](https://drive.google.com/drive/folders/1kCWXifyNegE5GBZXKlC-oh20U71H6HJi?usp=sharing)

## Reading Focus
This week turns toward authorship, intention, style, and co-creation.
The point is not to ask whether AI is creative in the abstract, but to ask how expressive work changes when generative systems participate in making.

## What To Look For
As you read, identify four things:

1. The role of the human maker
- What part of the expressive act belongs to the human?

2. The role of the system
- Is AI functioning as collaborator, instrument, material, filter, or provocation?

3. The legibility of authorship
- How does a design preserve, reshape, or obscure human intention and style?

4. A project implication
- What kind of expression is your project supporting, transforming, or complicating?

## Crucial Questions
Come to class ready to discuss:

1. What part of this expressive act belongs to the human?
2. What part can be delegated, transformed, or destabilized by AI?
3. Is the system supporting creativity, or substituting for it in ways that hide human intention?
4. How does your project preserve or rethink authorship?
5. What constraints, prompts, or interactions make expression more deliberate rather than less?

## Weekly Structure
Each week in this part of the course, you do the same three things:

1. Complete the assigned readings.
2. Come prepared to discuss them.
3. Bring a short `Project Throughline` update.

## Project Throughline
Bring a short weekly update that responds to these prompts:

- `This week's question for my project is/was...`
- `One design decision I made because of it is/was...`
- `One thing I tested, sketched, or represented is/was...`
- `What changed in my project thinking is/was...`

This does not need to be code.
It can be a sketch, diagram, prompt flow, prototype, comparison, storyboard, or another concrete representation of your thinking.

## Possible Outcome
By the end of class, you should be able to describe how your project handles authorship, intention, co-creation, and expressive control.
