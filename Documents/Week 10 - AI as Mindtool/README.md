# Week 10 - AI as Mindtool

## Core Question
**When should AI help people think rather than think for them?**

## Reading Assignment
Read the following before class:

1. Jonassen, D. H., Carr, C., and Yueh, H.-P. (1998). *Computers as mindtools for engaging learners in critical thinking.*
2. Clark, A. (2025). *Extending minds with generative AI.*
3. Vendrell, M., and Johnston, S.-K. (2026). *Scaffolding critical thinking with generative AI: Design principles for integrating large language models in higher education.*
4. Dede, C., Etemadi, A., and Forshaw, T. (2021). *Intelligence augmentation: Upskilling humans to complement AI.*

Course readings folder: [Google Drive](https://drive.google.com/drive/folders/1kCWXifyNegE5GBZXKlC-oh20U71H6HJi?usp=sharing)

## Reading Focus
This week asks what kinds of thinking are worth preserving and designing for.
Rather than treating AI as an answer-machine, consider when it acts as a support for inquiry, comparison, judgment, reflection, or problem-solving.

## What To Look For
As you read, identify four things:

1. The kind of problem
- What kind of problem or task is the human actually working on?

2. The cognitive role of the tool
- Is AI functioning as answer-machine, scaffold, comparison engine, coach, or mindtool?

3. The boundary of support
- What kind of thinking should the AI support, and what kind should it not take over?

4. A project implication
- What kind of thinking does your project invite, and how should AI participate in that process?

## Crucial Questions
Come to class ready to discuss:

1. What kind of problem is this system asking a person to work on?
2. What kind of thinking should the AI support?
3. What kind of thinking should the AI not take over?
4. What would be lost if the system solved the problem too fully for the user?
5. How can you describe your project in terms of cognitive engagement, not just features?

## Weekly Structure
Each week in this part of the course, you do the same three things:

1. Complete the assigned readings.
2. Come prepared to discuss them.
3. Bring a short `Project Throughline` update.

## Project Throughline
Bring a short weekly update that responds to these prompts:

- `This week's question for my project is/was...`
- `One design decision I made because of it is/was...`
- `One thing I tested, sketched, or represented is/was...`
- `What changed in my project thinking is/was...`

This does not need to be code.
It can be a sketch, diagram, prompt flow, prototype, comparison, storyboard, or another concrete representation of your thinking.

## Possible Outcome
By the end of class, you should be better able to describe your project in terms of the thinking, judgment, or problem-solving it supports.
