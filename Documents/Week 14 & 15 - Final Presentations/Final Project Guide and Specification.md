# Final Project Guide and Specification

## Overview
For the final project, you will design, implement, and reflect on a human-AI system, interaction, or experience that explores a meaningful boundary question.

Your project can take different forms, but it should be grounded in a real inquiry.
It should make clear:
- what phenomenon, question, or tension you are exploring,
- what the AI is doing,
- what remains meaningfully human,
- and why the project matters.

You can build different kinds of projects, including:
- a prototype,
- an interactive tool,
- a speculative system,
- an expressive or interpretive artifact,
- a prompt-based workflow,
- or another computational experience that fits your question.

The main requirement is not polish for its own sake.
The main requirement is clarity of inquiry: your project should show that you used making, testing, and reflection to investigate a boundary question.

## Boundary Inquiry
Your project should be legible as an inquiry into:
- learning,
- agency,
- interpretation,
- expression,
- experience,
- or another closely related human question at the AI boundary.

You should be able to explain:
- what human phenomenon or question the project addresses,
- what the AI is doing,
- what remains human,
- what design choices were made and why,
- and what the project reveals through making, testing, or staging.

## Required Parts
Your final project should include three connected parts:

### 1. Conceptual framing
A short explanation of the question, phenomenon, or boundary your project investigates, and the research, readings, or concepts that help frame it.

At minimum, use course readings and/or a small set of outside sources that meaningfully inform your design.

### 2. Computational artifact
Something you actually build.
This can be a working prototype, coded system, mock-up, staged interaction, or another computational artifact appropriate to your question.

### 3. Reflection
A short reflection connecting your idea, your design decisions, and what you learned through building, testing, or staging the project.

## Deliverables
Submit the following:

### 1. Project artifact
Your actual build, prototype, system, or staged experience.

Include links to the project materials when relevant, such as:
- a GitHub repository,
- a live prototype,
- a video walkthrough,
- supporting media,
- or another location where the work can be accessed or examined.

### 2. Final project write-up
Submit one combined write-up as a Google Doc of about 1500 words total.

This write-up should include:
- a short project statement,
- evidence or process documentation,
- and a final reflection.

Roughly, the write-up should help a reader understand:
- your guiding question,
- your conceptual frame,
- what the system does,
- what remains human,
- what you built and tested,
- what changed as the project developed,
- and what the project ultimately made visible.

At the top of the document, clearly include:
- project title,
- your name or group members' names,
- and URLs to the project artifact(s), such as code repositories, live prototypes, or videos.

At the end of the document, include references.

Images, screenshots, and similar supporting material can be included in an appendix and do not count toward the word count.

Evidence or process documentation can take different forms depending on the project.
Include some form of documentation that helps show how the project developed, what you tested, or what the project made visible.

This might include:
- screenshots or interface states,
- prompt iterations or output comparisons,
- sketches, diagrams, or storyboards,
- notes from trying the system,
- observations from use or testing,
- examples of revisions or changes in direction,
- brief excerpts from interactions,
- or another form of documentation appropriate to your project.

## Rule of Thumb
If you present your project, you should be able to answer:
- Why does this matter?
- What boundary question is this investigating?
- Why is AI part of this system?
- What remains human?
- What did you learn by building or testing it?

## Connection to Weeks 09-13
Your project does not need to answer every course question from Weeks 09-13.

But it should be shaped by at least some of them, such as:
- What should remain human?
- What kind of thinking is worth designing for?
- What kind of meaning should remain open?
- What kind of expression is being supported or transformed?
- What kind of experience is being staged?

You do not need to cover all five.
One or two may matter much more for your project than the others.

## Collaboration
Projects may be individual or collaborative.

If you are working collaboratively, your submission should still make clear:
- who is in the group,
- what the shared project is,
- and how the written submission explains the project's inquiry, design, and learning.

If the project is collaborative, submit one shared write-up with all authors' names clearly listed.

## Final Presentation
In Weeks 14 and 15, you will present your project to the class.

Your presentation should help others understand:
- the question your project explores,
- what you built,
- what the AI does,
- what remains human,
- what you found through making or testing,
- and what the project reveals.
